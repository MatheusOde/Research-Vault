# A Design Science Research Methodology for Information Systems Research

## Summary

- Peffers, Tuunanen, Rothenberger, and Chatterjee propose a [[wiki/concepts/Design Science Research|Design Science Research]] methodology for information systems research.
- The paper argues that IS needed a shared process and presentation template because design research was legitimate but weakly diffused in mainstream IS outlets.
- The proposed DSRM process has six activities: problem identification and motivation, objectives of a solution, design and development, demonstration, evaluation, and communication.
- The process is nominally sequential but can start from four entry points: problem-centered, objective-centered, design/development-centered, or client/context-initiated.
- The authors demonstrate the DSRM by retroactively mapping four prior IS design studies to the process.

## Key Claims

- [[wiki/concepts/Design Science Research|DSR]] should produce and evaluate artifacts that solve identified organizational problems.
- A methodology needs principles, practice rules, and procedure; prior IS work had principles and guidelines, but not a consensus process model.
- A shared DSRM helps researchers, reviewers, and editors distinguish design research from consulting, poor empirical research, or practice case reports.
- Evaluation may use empirical evidence or logical proof, depending on artifact and venue.
- DSRM should not become rigid orthodoxy; authors explicitly allow other valid DSR methodologies and context-specific adaptations.

## Method Extracted

- Problem identification and motivation: define specific research problem and justify value of solution.
- Define objectives for solution: infer feasible, measurable or qualitative solution objectives from problem and known solutions.
- Design and development: create artifact, including construct, model, method, instantiation, or designed technical/social/informational property.
- Demonstration: show artifact solving one or more problem instances.
- Evaluation: compare solution objectives with observed results from demonstration.
- Communication: report problem, artifact, utility, novelty, rigor, and effectiveness to scholarly and practitioner audiences.
