# Conducting Systematic Literature Review In Operations Management

## Summary
- Proposes an eight-step SLR approach for operations management and studies SLR diffusion in OM.
- Steps: planning/formulating problem, searching literature, data gathering, quality evaluation, data analysis/synthesis, interpretation, presenting results, and updating review.
- Argues that OM lacks detailed SLR guidance and that OM reviews often do not meet methodological rigor.
- Finds growth in OM literature reviews, especially meta-synthesis, but gaps in quality appraisal, coding reliability, and publication-bias reporting.

## Details
The paper distinguishes narrative reviews, meta-synthesis, and meta-analysis. It argues SLRs differ from narrative reviews by explicit protocols, comprehensive search, replicability, and audit trails.

Planning includes defining a team, checking whether a review is needed, defining scope, identifying research topics and questions, operationalizing variables/categories, and writing a protocol. Search includes database/journal selection, keyword search, abstract review, inclusion/exclusion criteria, full-text review, backward search, and forward search.

Data gathering is organized through coding schemes, codebooks, and study/concept matrices. Quality evaluation covers publication bias, methodological bias, selective reporting, and validity criteria. The paper stresses reliability checks across study selection and coding.

Its empirical example found that SLR use in OM increased after 2010, but many reviews lacked detailed search keywords, backward/forward search reporting, multiple coders, intercoder reliability, quality appraisal, and publication-bias assessment.
