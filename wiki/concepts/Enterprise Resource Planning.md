# Enterprise Resource Planning

## Summary
- ERP is best treated as both packaged application software and an enterprise integration infrastructure, not only as a technical product.
- Core characteristics across sources: packaged and configurable software, integrated database, broad process coverage, process orientation, multi-industry and multi-country support, and recurring transaction support.
- ERP evolved from MRP, MRP II, and CIM toward broader process and data integration, then toward extended, web, cloud, mobile, and Industry 4.0-enabled ERP.
- Claims about ERP benefits should distinguish implementation completion from post-implementation organizational performance.

## Details
Klaus, Rosemann, and Gable argue that a single accepted ERP definition was not yet stable in 2000, but identify three views: ERP as software commodity, ERP as a development objective for integrated enterprise process/data mapping, and ERP as infrastructure for business solutions. Their most useful framing for this vault is the third view.

Bahssas et al. describe ERP architecture changes from two-tier and three-tier client/server systems toward web-based, six-tier, cloud, and mobile ERP. Al-Amin et al. extend this evolution toward AI, BI, IoT, blockchain, big data, omnichannel strategy, cloud ERP, and cybersecurity concerns.
