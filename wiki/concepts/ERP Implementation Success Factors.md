# ERP Implementation Success Factors

## Summary
- ERP success factors are not automatically critical; Ram et al. argue they must be tested against implementation success and post-implementation performance separately.
- Recurring factors across sources include project management, training and education, business process reengineering, system integration, top management support, communication, team capability, monitoring, and vendor/consultant support.
- Knowledge transfer and relationship quality matter because ERP projects require bidirectional translation between packaged-system knowledge and local business-process knowledge.
- Reference models and enterprise modelling can reduce consultant over-dependence by making goals, rules, actors, resources, processes, concepts, and technical requirements explicit.

## Details
Ram et al. empirically separate ERP implementation success from organizational performance improvement. This matters because a project can finish on time/budget/user expectations yet still fail to create later operational, financial, or customer-service benefits.

Hung et al. show that a positive knowledge transfer climate mediates ERP knowledge transfer. Supported bonding factors include top management support, internal incentives, consultant industry experience, and consultant project management capability; interdepartmental coordination and consultant reward system were not supported in their model.

Carvalho and Guerrini frame ERP implementation as an innovation-agent network involving customer, supplier, and consulting service. Their EKD-based reference model is designed to internalize knowledge, define responsibilities, create KPIs, support change-driven processes, and prevent future underuse or maintenance dependency.
